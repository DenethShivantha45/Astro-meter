Mercury

Type: Terrestrial Planet

Distance from Star: 57.9 million km (0.39 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • Smallest and innermost planet.
  • Extremely thin exosphere, no true atmosphere.
  • Experiences extreme temperature swings (-173°C to 427°C).
  • Heavily cratered surface, similar to Earth's Moon.
  • No known natural satellites.

(Data updated as of: )

Venus

Type: Terrestrial Planet

Distance from Star: 108.2 million km (0.72 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • Second planet from the Sun, often called Earth's 'sister planet'.
  • Thick, toxic atmosphere of sulfuric acid clouds.
  • Hottest planet in the Solar System (~462°C) due to runaway greenhouse effect.
  • Rotates in the opposite direction to most planets (retrograde rotation).
  • No known natural satellites.

(Data updated as of: )

Earth

Type: Terrestrial Planet

Distance from Star: 149.6 million km (1 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • The only planet known to harbor life.
  • Atmosphere rich in nitrogen and oxygen.
  • About 71% of its surface is covered by liquid water.
  • Has one natural satellite: the Moon.
  • Magnetic field protects from solar wind.

(Data updated as of: )

Mars

Type: Terrestrial Planet

Distance from Star: 227.9 million km (1.52 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • The 'Red Planet' due to iron oxide on its surface.
  • Thin atmosphere, mostly carbon dioxide.
  • Features polar ice caps, volcanoes, and canyons.
  • Has two small moons: Phobos and Deimos.
  • Evidence suggests presence of liquid water in the distant past.

(Data updated as of: )

Jupiter

Type: Gas Giant

Distance from Star: 778.5 million km (5.20 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • Largest planet in our Solar System.
  • Known for its Great Red Spot, a persistent storm larger than Earth.
  • Composed mainly of hydrogen and helium.
  • Has a powerful magnetic field.
  • Possesses a faint ring system and 95 known moons.

(Data updated as of: )

Saturn

Type: Gas Giant

Distance from Star: 1.434 billion km (9.58 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • Famous for its prominent, complex ring system.
  • Second largest planet in the Solar System.
  • Lower density than water, it would float if placed in a large enough ocean.
  • Has 146 known moons, including the large moon Titan with a thick atmosphere.
  • Strong winds and auroras in its polar regions.

(Data updated as of: )

Uranus

Type: Ice Giant

Distance from Star: 2.871 billion km (19.23 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • Rotates on its side, with its axis tilted almost parallel to its orbit.
  • Atmosphere appears blue-green due to methane absorption.
  • Has a faint ring system and 27 known moons.
  • Experiences extreme seasonal variations due to its axial tilt.

(Data updated as of: )

Neptune

Type: Ice Giant

Distance from Star: 4.495 billion km (30.10 AU) from Sun

Characteristics:

  • Farthest known planet from the Sun.
  • Known for the fastest winds in the Solar System.
  • Has dynamic storm systems, though less persistent than Jupiter's.
  • Has 14 known moons, including Triton which orbits in a retrograde direction.
  • Deep blue color, likely due to methane and other unknown constituents.

(Data updated as of: )

Proxima Centauri b

Type: Terrestrial Exoplanet (Super-Earth)

Distance from Star: 7.5 million km (0.05 AU) from Proxima Centauri

Characteristics:

  • Orbits Proxima Centauri, the closest star to our Sun.
  • Potentially habitable, lying within its star's habitable zone.
  • Believed to be tidally locked, with one side always facing its star.
  • Mass is at least 1.17 times that of Earth.

(Data updated as of: )

TRAPPIST-1e

Type: Terrestrial Exoplanet

Distance from Star: 4.2 million km (0.028 AU) from TRAPPIST-1

Characteristics:

  • One of seven Earth-sized planets in the TRAPPIST-1 system.
  • Located in the habitable zone, potentially capable of supporting liquid water.
  • Likely tidally locked to its ultra-cool dwarf star.
  • Part of a fascinating system with planets very close to each other.

(Data updated as of: )

Kepler-186f

Type: Terrestrial Exoplanet

Distance from Star: 52.4 million km (0.35 AU) from Kepler-186

Characteristics:

  • First Earth-size planet discovered in the habitable zone of another star.
  • Orbits a red dwarf star, slightly cooler and smaller than our Sun.
  • Its size is estimated to be only 10% larger than Earth.
  • Could potentially have liquid water on its surface.

(Data updated as of: )

HD 209458 b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet ('Hot Jupiter')

Distance from Star: 7 million km (0.047 AU) from HD 209458

Characteristics:

  • First exoplanet discovered to transit its star, allowing atmospheric studies.
  • A 'Hot Jupiter' due to its large size and extremely close orbit.
  • Its atmosphere shows evidence of water vapor, sodium, and carbon monoxide.
  • Experiences extreme temperatures and strong winds.

(Data updated as of: )

WASP-12b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet ('Hot Jupiter')

Distance from Star: 3.4 million km (0.023 AU) from WASP-12

Characteristics:

  • One of the hottest and fastest-orbiting exoplanets known.
  • So close to its star that it's being tidally stretched into an egg shape.
  • Losing its atmosphere to its star at a rapid rate.
  • Atmosphere contains significant amounts of carbon.

(Data updated as of: )

Gliese 504 b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet (Super-Jupiter)

Distance from Star: 7.3 billion km (43.5 AU) from Gliese 504

Characteristics:

  • One of the lowest-mass exoplanets ever directly imaged.
  • Has a distinct magenta color, possibly due to atmospheric composition.
  • Orbits a relatively young star.
  • Considered a 'super-Jupiter' due to its large size.

(Data updated as of: )

HR 8799 e

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet

Distance from Star: 2.2 billion km (14.5 AU) from HR 8799

Characteristics:

  • One of four large exoplanets directly imaged orbiting the star HR 8799.
  • Still very young and hot, radiating heat from its formation.
  • Its atmosphere contains methane and carbon monoxide.
  • Provides a unique opportunity to study planetary formation.

(Data updated as of: )

CoRoT-7b

Type: Terrestrial Exoplanet (Super-Earth)

Distance from Star: 2.5 million km (0.017 AU) from CoRoT-7

Characteristics:

  • First rocky exoplanet discovered outside our solar system.
  • Extremely close to its star, resulting in scorching temperatures (up to 2,500°C).
  • May have 'rain' of vaporized rock that condenses at higher altitudes.
  • Likely tidally locked, with a permanent day and night side.

(Data updated as of: )

K2-18b

Type: Mini-Neptune Exoplanet

Distance from Star: 22 million km (0.14 AU) from K2-18

Characteristics:

  • Potentially contains liquid water clouds and rain.
  • Considered a 'sub-Neptune' or 'mini-Neptune' with a significant atmosphere.
  • Orbits a red dwarf star in its habitable zone.
  • Initial observations suggest water vapor in its atmosphere.

(Data updated as of: )

TOI-164b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet (Hot Jupiter)

Distance from Star: 10.5 million km (0.07 AU) from TOI-164

Characteristics:

  • A relatively recently discovered 'Hot Jupiter'.
  • Its discovery highlights the power of the TESS mission in finding transiting exoplanets.
  • Typical of this class, with a short orbital period and high temperatures.

(Data updated as of: )

51 Pegasi b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet ('Hot Jupiter')

Distance from Star: 7.8 million km (0.05 AU) from 51 Pegasi

Characteristics:

  • The first exoplanet discovered orbiting a sun-like star (in 1995).
  • Revolutionized our understanding of planetary systems.
  • A 'Hot Jupiter' that challenges previous theories of planet formation.
  • Demonstrated that giant planets can exist in very close orbits.

(Data updated as of: )

Gliese 581 g

Type: Terrestrial Exoplanet (Super-Earth)

Distance from Star: 22 million km (0.14 AU) from Gliese 581

Characteristics:

  • Considered a strong candidate for habitability (though its existence is debated).
  • If it exists, it would orbit a red dwarf star in its habitable zone.
  • Estimated to be 3-4 times the mass of Earth.
  • Its discovery spurred significant interest in finding habitable worlds.

(Data updated as of: )

HD 189733 b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet ('Hot Jupiter')

Distance from Star: 4.8 million km (0.031 AU) from HD 189733

Characteristics:

  • Known for its deep blue color, likely due to silicate particles in its atmosphere.
  • Experiences supersonic winds, reaching up to 8,700 km/h.
  • Has a very hot atmosphere, with temperatures around 1,000°C.
  • Frequently observed for atmospheric studies due to its transiting nature.

(Data updated as of: )

G 9-40 c

Type: Super-Earth/Mini-Neptune Exoplanet

Distance from Star: 7.5 million km (0.05 AU) from G 9-40

Characteristics:

  • One of the first multi-planet systems found by the TESS mission.
  • This system has two planets, with 'c' being the outer one.
  • Offers insights into the formation and evolution of multi-planet systems around M-dwarfs.

Characteristics:

  • Part of a two-planet system discovered around a cool star.
  • Likely a rocky planet, slightly larger than Earth.

  • Part of a system with at least four planets.

TOI 700 d

Distance from Star: 27 million km (0.16 AU) from TOI 700

Characteristics:

  • Earth-sized exoplanet orbiting within the habitable zone of its star.
  • Discovered by NASA's TESS mission.
  • Its star, TOI 700, is a small, cool M-dwarf star.
  • Considered a strong candidate for follow-up studies to search for biosignatures.

KELT-9b

Type: Ultra-Hot Jupiter Exoplanet

Distance from Star: 5.1 million km (0.034 AU) from KELT-9

Characteristics:

  • The hottest known exoplanet, with a dayside temperature over 4,300°C.
  • Hotter than some stars, its atmosphere is being vaporized.
  • Orbits a very hot, massive star.
  • Its atmosphere contains exotic gases like atomic hydrogen.

VHS 1256 b

Type: Brown Dwarf/Sub-Brown Dwarf

Distance from Star: 12 billion km (80 AU) from VHS 1256

Characteristics:

  • Not a planet in the traditional sense, but a 'planetary mass object' or brown dwarf.
  • Known for its highly dynamic and stormy atmosphere with silicate clouds.
  • Directly imaged, allowing detailed study of its atmosphere.
  • Exhibits significant brightness variations due to its weather patterns.

(Data updated as of: )

WASP-39b

Type: Gas Giant Exoplanet (Hot Saturn)

Distance from Star: 6.8 million km (0.045 AU) from WASP-39

Characteristics:

  • Known for being the first exoplanet with definitive evidence of carbon dioxide in its atmosphere (detected by JWST).
  • A 'Hot Saturn' - a gas giant with a density similar to Saturn but much closer to its star.
  • Puffy atmosphere, making it an excellent target for atmospheric characterization.
  • Highlights the power of new telescopes in understanding exoplanet atmospheres.
